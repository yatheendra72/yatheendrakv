I grew up in a household where God was not a question. God was a given — built into the rhythm of the week, the names we called on, the explanations offered for the things that couldn't be explained otherwise.

At some point, as it does for a lot of people in my generation, the question cracked open. Not dramatically. Just quietly, over years, as education and reading and the general project of thinking for yourself started to apply pressure to things I'd never examined.

And yet — here's the strange part — the question won't close.

## What I mean by that

I'm not arguing for a particular answer here. I don't know what I believe, and I've made my peace with that uncertainty. What I find interesting is something prior to the answer: the *persistence* of the question.

The materialist case is, in many ways, very strong. The scientific account of the universe is staggeringly successful. The specific claims of religious traditions — miracles, interventions, divine authorship of texts — are, at best, hard to defend.

And yet. When I'm completely honest about my experience of being alive — the irreducible weirdness of consciousness, the sense that things *matter*, the feeling in certain moments that something is present — the tidy materialist story starts to feel like it's leaving a lot on the floor.

I'm not saying that gap is God. I'm saying I don't think it's nothing.

## The people who seem most awake

I've noticed something, which might be confirmation bias but I don't think so: the people I've encountered who seem most *present* — most alive to their experience, most genuinely at peace — tend to have some serious relationship with the vertical dimension of existence. Whether that's religious practice, contemplative discipline, or just a sustained engagement with the question.

Maybe that's coincidence. Maybe it's selection. Maybe it's that the question itself, taken seriously, does something to you that has nothing to do with the answer.

I keep coming back because I think it might be the most important question there is. Even if — especially if — it doesn't resolve.
