Every morning I strap something to my wrist that tells time less accurately than a three-dollar quartz watch. It sweeps instead of ticks. It might lose thirty seconds a week. It requires winding, or the kinetic energy of my arm, just to keep running. And it costs more than some cars.

I do this because I love it. But love is not a sufficient explanation — it's just a label we put on the thing we haven't explained yet.

## The honest question

A smart person, confronted with mechanical watch enthusiasm for the first time, would reasonably ask: why? Your phone is in your pocket. It shows the time to the millisecond, synced to atomic clocks via satellite, and it also lets you order groceries.

The mechanical watch has exactly one job, and it does that job worse.

So the question isn't "do you like watches" — lots of people like things. The question is what's really going on when a person finds themselves caring deeply about a tiny machine strapped to their wrist.

## What I think is actually happening

Mechanical watches are one of the last consumer objects that make their inner life visible. Lift the caseback and the movement is running — gears meshing, springs winding and releasing, the balance wheel oscillating four or eight times a second in its endless tick-tock. It's doing real physical work, right there.

Most things we own are opaque. The phone is a black slab of glass doing something incomprehensible. The car's engine is buried under plastic covers. The watch shows you everything. This transparency feels almost radical now.

## Time as object

There's also something philosophically interesting about wearing time on your body.

We talk about time constantly — not having enough of it, wasting it, making the most of it. But we rarely encounter it as a *thing*. The mechanical watch makes time physical. It has weight. It takes up space. It has a heartbeat you can feel with your thumb.

I don't think this is sentimental nonsense. Objects shape attention. A watch on your wrist is a persistent, gentle reminder that time is passing — that hours are real, that days are finite. Checking a phone tells you the time but also offers ten other things competing for your attention. The watch just tells you the time.

## The craft argument

Finally: watchmaking is extraordinarily difficult, and the difficulty is part of the point.

A top-grade movement contains hundreds of components machined to tolerances measured in microns, assembled by hand, regulated to accuracy standards that defy the physics of the materials involved. The people who do this work have spent years learning it. Some complications — a perpetual calendar, a minute repeater, a tourbillon — represent decades of refinement by dozens of engineers.

When you wear a watch, you're wearing the crystallized effort of extraordinary human skill. That's not nothing.

---

I'm aware this sounds like rationalization. Maybe it is. But I think the rationalization points at something real: that in a world of abundant digital convenience, there's meaning in choosing objects that are slow, physical, and difficult. That push back a little.

The watch on my wrist is not convenient. That's exactly the point.
